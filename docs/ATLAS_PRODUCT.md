# Atlas Product

## Product Intent

Atlas is an AI investment partner for long-term investors.

The intended experience is not a terminal full of isolated reports. Atlas
should feel like a calm investment briefing system that has already reviewed
the investor's profile, portfolio, watchlists, market context, evidence, and
prior decisions before speaking.

Atlas should fit naturally into the investor's workflow:

- Start the day with context.
- Review what changed.
- Study ideas without urgency.
- Compare alternatives.
- Record reasoning before decisions.
- Review decisions after evidence changes.
- Improve decision quality over time.

## Home

Home is the user's investment briefing surface.

It should answer:

- What matters right now?
- Is anything meaningfully different?
- What should I monitor?
- Is my portfolio still aligned with my profile?
- What questions are worth asking next?

Home should not be a grid of disconnected widgets. It should be a synthesized
briefing that connects investor context, portfolio state, market environment,
themes, monitoring signals, and suggested questions.

## Daily Brief

Daily Brief should help the user feel informed without feeling overwhelmed.

It is not a news feed and should not reward constant checking. It should
summarize meaningful changes, stable conditions, risks to watch, and research
directions. Most days, the correct tone may be calm and uneventful.

Daily Brief should help the user distinguish between:

- Important changes.
- Normal noise.
- Signals worth monitoring.
- Areas where no action is needed.

## Portfolio Review

Portfolio Review should feel like a CIO-style review of the whole portfolio.

It is not a performance report and not a buy or sell engine. It should explain
alignment between the portfolio, investor profile, market context, risk
capacity, time horizon, and portfolio purpose.

It should surface:

- Portfolio strengths.
- Main risks.
- Concentration.
- Suitability.
- Theme exposure.
- Market context.
- Missing information.
- Questions that could materially change the assessment.

## Watchlist Review

Watchlist Review should help the user separate signal from noise.

A good watchlist is not simply a long list of ideas. It should be useful,
focused, evidence-aware, and connected to the investor's goals. Atlas should
identify ideas that appear relevant, ideas worth monitoring, ideas requiring
better evidence, and ideas that may be driven mostly by hype or duplication.

The tone should remain respectful. Atlas should never mock the user's ideas.

## Investment Comparison

Investment Comparison should help the user compare alternatives with a
consistent framework.

The comparison experience should clarify differences in quality, valuation,
growth, risk, confidence, evidence, and fit. It should explain tradeoffs rather
than produce a simplistic winner.

The best comparison reduces confusion by making the choice architecture visible.

## Decision Journal

Decision Journal preserves the reasoning behind investment decisions.

It is not a trade tracker. It captures the thesis, assumptions, evidence
quality, risks, Atlas assessment, monitoring plan, and review triggers at the
time a decision was considered.

The journal should teach the investor that:

- A good decision can have a bad outcome.
- A bad decision can have a good outcome.
- Decision quality matters more than outcome alone.
- Learning requires preserving the original context.

## Weekly Review

Weekly Review should become the investor's recurring reflection habit.

It should summarize what changed across the portfolio, watchlists, market
health, economic signals, themes, risk drift, and decision journal. It should
help the user decide what deserves study, what deserves patience, and what
should be reviewed more deeply.

Weekly Review should avoid creating activity for its own sake.

## Discovery

Discovery should help investors find research directions, not receive
personalized instructions.

Atlas should identify themes, bottlenecks, second-order effects, industries,
companies, ETFs, assets, and evidence gaps. Discovery should be grounded in
structured reasoning and should make clear when evidence is preliminary.

The product should encourage curiosity without encouraging speculation.

## Long-Term Investor Journey

Atlas should become more valuable as the investor uses it over time.

The long-term journey should move through:

1. Understanding the investor profile.
2. Understanding the portfolio.
3. Monitoring market and economic context.
4. Reviewing watchlists and themes.
5. Comparing investment alternatives.
6. Recording decision reasoning.
7. Reviewing decisions after time passes.
8. Learning which decisions were high quality.
9. Refining future reasoning.

Atlas should help users become better investors, not simply more active ones.
